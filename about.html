About Us

The first significant step toward the development of gastronomy was the use of fire by primitive humans to cook food, which gave rise to the first meals as families gathered around the fire to share the foods they had cooked. Prehistoric cave paintings such as those in Les Trois Frères in Ariège, in southern France, depict these early gastronomic events. In the ancient civilizations of Assyria, Babylonia, Persia, and Egypt, the selection, preparation, service, and enjoyment of food were practiced on an elaborate scale. In the Book of Daniel the Bible relates the story of how Belshazzar, the king of the Chaldeans, “made a great feast to a thousand of his lords, and drank wine before the thousand.” He then commanded gold and silver vessels to be brought, and he and his wives, princes, and concubines drank wine and praised gods of gold, silver, brass, iron, wood, and stone.

Greece and Rome In ancient Greece, the Athenians believed that mealtime afforded an opportunity to nourish the spirit as well as the body. They reclined on couches while eating and accompanied their repasts with music, poetry, and dancing. The Greeks provided a philosophical basis for good living, Epicureanism. It held that pleasure was the main purpose of life, but pleasure was not intended to imply the self-indulgence that it connotes today. The Epicureans believed that pleasure could best be achieved by practicing self-restraint and indulging as few desires as possible. Today the epicure is defined as one who is “endowed with sensitive and discriminating tastes in food and wine.”

Welcome to Gastronomy's World.

On Saturday, November 12, the Food Society Paris, located between the rue du Maine and the avenue du Commandant-René-Mouchotte in the 14th arrondissement, south of Paris, celebrated its launch. Its finely tuned communication strategy on Instagram and TikTok brought in cohorts of young people curious to discover the venue. Under multi-colored beams, they wandered around the place with a beer in one hand and their cell phone in the other to collect their order at one of the 15 kiosks of this 3,500 square meter food court, one of the biggest in Europe. If those in the United States would not find this concept of grouped catering with continuous service surprising, it is just starting to take root in the country of bistros and brasseries.

It's not even midday, but a line has already formed in front of one of the Parisian outlets of O'Tacos, the French sandwich giant with more than 300 establishments. There are clusters of very young teenagers among the customers, but also some 40-year-olds who have lost their way. And there is plenty of reason to do so. Here, the tortilla can be folded around just about any ingredient: cordon bleu, merguez and even raclette cheese. These culinary collisions wreak havoc on the palate, even though the taco's flavor is invariably drowned in a mysterious "cheese sauce" that includes a healthy dose of cheddar. O'Tacos, which does not go in for gourmet frills, even features a "gigataco" the size of an infant and comprising over 2.5 kilograms of meat.
